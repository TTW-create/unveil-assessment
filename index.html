Stage 1 of 6 · U
Uncover Your Story
Purpose: To understand where you are in your career journey and honor the path that brought you here. There is no right timeline. Nonlinear paths are welcome here.
Question 1 of 24
In a few sentences, describe where you are right now in your career or professional life.
It is okay if the answer is "I don't know." That is a completely valid and honest starting point.
Question 2 of 24
Think about a turning point or pivot in your life that changed how you think about work. What happened, and what did you learn about yourself?
This can be big or small. You are not being asked about trauma, just about turning points, which are part of every human story.
Question 3 of 24
When you imagine your professional life one year from now, what does a good day look like? Describe it in as much detail as you can.
Think in sensory detail rather than job titles. Where are you? Who are you with? What are you doing?
Question 4 of 24
What is one thing about your career journey that you wish people understood? Something that is not obvious from a resume.
This surfaces hidden narratives and lived experience that deserve to be honored.
Stage 2 of 6 · N
Name Your Strengths & Values
Purpose: To identify what you naturally do well and what matters most to you. Career satisfaction lives at the intersection of capability and meaning.
Question 5 of 24
What do people consistently come to you for, at work, at school, or in life? What role do you naturally play in groups?
This identifies your observable strengths through external validation patterns.
Question 6 of 24
Think of a time you helped someone or solved a problem and felt genuinely proud. What were you doing, and what skill made you effective?
Strengths revealed through stories are more reliable than self-rating scales.

Question 8 of 24
What is something you are good at that you do not enjoy doing? And what is something you love doing even if you are still building skill in it?
This separates competence from passion and prevents career paths that feel like golden handcuffs.
Stage 3 of 6 · V
Verify Your Environment
Purpose: To understand what conditions you need to do your best work. Your nervous system knows what environments help you thrive. This stage helps you name it.
Question 9 of 24
Think about a time you felt energized and focused at work or school. Describe the environment. Was it quiet or active? Structured or flexible? Alone or collaborative?
This identifies your optimal conditions from lived experience, not just preference.
Question 10 of 24
Now think about a time you felt drained, anxious, or disengaged. What was different about that environment?
This identifies misalignment without requiring you to disclose anything deeply personal.

Question 12 of 24
What does psychological safety at work look like to you?
For example: being able to ask questions, making mistakes without punishment, being valued for who you are.
Stage 4 of 6 · E
Edit Your Narrative
Purpose: To practice telling your professional story in a way that is honest, empowering, and forward-looking. This stage helps you take the pen back.
Question 13 of 24
What is a story you have told yourself about your career that holds you back?
Examples: "I am too old to start over." "I do not have the right degree." Naming the limiting story is the first step to rewriting it.
Question 14 of 24
Now rewrite that story. If a trusted friend or mentor were describing your career journey, what would they say instead?
Shift from self-criticism to a compassionate, honest reframe.
Question 15 of 24
What is a professional challenge you have faced that you now see differently? Something that felt like a failure but taught you something valuable.
Adversity is data, not identity. What did that experience teach you about yourself?
Question 16 of 24
If you had to introduce yourself to a room of people you respect, in two sentences without using your job title, what would you say?
Who are you at your professional core, beyond your role?
Stage 5 of 6 · I
Invest in Your Goals
Purpose: To set goals that are real, rooted in your current life, and connected to who you are becoming, not who you think you should be.
Question 17 of 24
Right now, what feels like the most important career question you need to answer?
This might be "What do I want to do?" or "How do I get unstuck?" or "How do I translate what I know into something an employer values?"
Question 18 of 24
What would progress look like to you in the next 90 days? Be as specific as you can.
Not "get a job" but maybe "have three informational interviews and a revised resume." Specificity makes goals real.
Question 19 of 24
What is the biggest barrier standing between you and that progress? Is it internal (fear, confidence, clarity) or external (resources, access, time)?
Naming obstacles honestly is critical for realistic action planning.
Question 20 of 24
On a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that you can take a meaningful step toward your career goals in the next 30 days?
Select your number, then answer: what would move that number up by 1?

Stage 6 of 6 · L
Launch Your Next Step
Purpose: To leave this assessment with one concrete, manageable action, not a life plan. Momentum starts with a single step.
Question 21 of 24
Based on everything you have reflected on, what is ONE thing you want to do in the next 7 days to move forward?
It can be small. Specificity doubles your chance of follow-through.
Question 22 of 24
What support would make that step easier?
Examples: an accountability partner, a specific resource, a conversation with someone, permission to try something imperfect.
Question 23 of 24
If you could ask a career professional one question right now, what would it be?
This ensures any follow-up conversation addresses what matters most to you.
